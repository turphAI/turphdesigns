** 2. Introduction

Definition of terms: searching vs. researching
Searching is a discrete, targeted activity focused on retrieving specific information with clear, well-defined queries and success measured by speed and precision. Researching, in contrast, is an iterative, exploratory process aimed at building knowledge through evolving queries across multiple sessions, with success measured by depth of understanding and confidence in decisions. Rather than existing as binary states, these activities form a continuum where users frequently transition between modes—often beginning with a simple search that evolves into deeper research as they discover the complexity of their information needs. Traditional interfaces force unnatural transitions between these states, creating friction particularly evident in financial contexts, where simple information retrieval typically serves as just the first step toward complex decision-making requiring iterative exploration and synthesis.

The opportunity for conversational interfaces to transform information access
Conversational interfaces are uniquely positioned to bridge the gap between searching and researching by adapting to users' fluid information needs. Unlike traditional search boxes that reset with each query, conversational systems maintain context across interactions, allowing natural progression from simple lookups to complex exploration. They can recognize when a user is transitioning from fact-finding to knowledge-building, automatically adjusting their response depth and suggesting related avenues of inquiry. By maintaining a persistent memory of previous exchanges, these interfaces eliminate the burden of context rebuilding while enabling users to easily mark important findings and discard irrelevant information. This memory-based approach creates a collaborative research environment where the system becomes an active partner in connecting disparate pieces of information, identifying patterns, and building toward meaningful conclusions—fundamentally transforming how users access, process, and apply complex information in decision-making processes.

** 3. Problem Statement

User challenges in transitioning between search and research modes
Users today interact with search interfaces as discrete, transactional experiences rather than continuous, evolving journeys. They approach each search interaction as isolated and ephemeral because there is no built-in mechanism or learned behavior connecting related queries into a coherent knowledge-building activity. The mental model of "search" has been conditioned by decades of simple query-response systems that lack memory or context.

Without intuitive ways to mark, prioritize, or dismiss information based on relevance to broader goals, users struggle to build upon their discoveries. The absence of project-based interfaces prevents users from organizing searches and findings into meaningful investigations or goals.

With no persistent representation of evolving information needs, each new session forces users to mentally rebuild context, placing the entire burden of connecting and curating information on the user. This fundamental disconnect limits how effectively users can transition from simple fact-finding to more complex research activities, particularly in domains like finance where decisions emerge from patterns and relationships developed over time.

Business impact of these limitations
As users become more familiar with project-based conversational experiences from other companies, they will increasingly demand similar capabilities. Organizations that fail to adapt, risk watching user engagement migrate to these more sophisticated interfaces where complex information needs can be met effectively. This migration threatens more than just traffic—it represents a critical loss of interaction data, relationship touchpoints, and opportunities to demonstrate domain expertise. Companies offering only fragmented, transactional search experiences will find themselves at a competitive disadvantage, unable to capture the behavioral insights that fuel personalization.


** 4. Background Research

Literature review: Information-seeking behavior patterns
Information-seeking behavior has been extensively studied across multiple disciplines, revealing patterns that are critical to understanding the search-research continuum. Carol Kuhlthau's Information Search Process (ISP) model identifies six stages users progress through: initiation, selection, exploration, formulation, collection, and presentation. This model demonstrates how uncertainty and anxiety decrease as users move from vague searching to focused researching, highlighting the emotional aspects of information seeking that interfaces must address.

Gary Marchionini's work distinguishes between "lookup" (fact-based) and "exploratory" (learning-based) search behaviors, noting that digital systems have historically prioritized lookup functions while underserving exploratory needs. His research shows that users engage in significantly different behaviors when seeking specific facts versus building understanding—employing different query strategies, evaluation criteria, and satisfaction thresholds.
Broder's taxonomy of web search categorizes queries as navigational, informational, or transactional, but fails to account for the iterative nature of complex information tasks. More recent work by Rose and Levinson expands this to recognize "resource" searches where users seek tools or materials to accomplish goals rather than just information—a pattern particularly relevant in financial contexts where users need not just data but actionable resources.
In financial domains specifically, Ellis and Haugan documented how professionals engage in "chaining" behavior—following citations and connections between information sources—a process poorly supported by traditional search interfaces but potentially well-suited to conversational approaches. Meanwhile, studies by O'Brien and Toms on user engagement show how maintaining flow states during information seeking correlates strongly with satisfaction and learning outcomes—states frequently disrupted when transitioning between search systems or sessions.

Understanding these established patterns provides crucial guidance for designing conversational interfaces that can effectively support the full spectrum of information-seeking behaviors, from simple lookups to complex, multi-session research processes.


Analysis of existing conversational interfaces
Current conversational interfaces demonstrate both promising capabilities and significant limitations in bridging the search-research gap. Leading platforms like Perplexity, ChatGPT, and Claude have introduced features that maintain conversation history and allow reference to previous exchanges, creating a foundation for continuous information exploration. Some offer the ability to save conversations or create project spaces, showing early recognition of research as an ongoing process. However, most still struggle with truly understanding when users are transitioning between search and research modes, often providing either overly simplistic answers to complex questions or overwhelming detail for straightforward queries.

The integration of search capabilities within these interfaces remains uneven—some effectively combine web retrieval with contextual understanding, while others either lack real-time information access or fail to meaningfully incorporate citations into their reasoning process. Few systems currently offer robust tools for users to explicitly signal which information is valuable to their broader goals or to organize findings into coherent knowledge structures. Additionally, domain-specific adaptations, particularly in regulated industries like finance, remain underdeveloped, with interfaces rarely adjusting their interaction patterns to match the complexity and compliance requirements of specialized information needs.

These gaps represent significant opportunities for innovation in designing conversational interfaces that can truly adapt to the full spectrum of information-seeking behaviors.

## User Expectations for Conversational Assistance

The evolution of conversational interfaces represents a fundamental paradigm shift in how users discover and process information. This transition demands careful consideration of both entry points and user psychology.

While traditional interfaces have conditioned users to begin with search-oriented discovery, conversational systems must support both search and research as valid entry points. Initially, most users will approach these systems with a discovery mindset, seeking specific information. However, once engaged, the interface should seamlessly transition to support deeper exploration without forcing users to explicitly switch modes or platforms. The challenge lies in designing interfaces that honor both the efficiency of search and the depth of research while maintaining a unified, intuitive experience.

For these new interfaces to gain widespread adoption, they must address two fundamental user needs: establishing trust and demonstrating immediate value. Users need assurance that their research activities are secure, private, and portable—that their data won't be used for training algorithms, shared without consent, or leveraged against their interests. Simultaneously, they require clear signals of value from their earliest interactions, with benefits that justify the cognitive investment of learning a new paradigm for information interaction.

The transition to conversation-first interfaces represents not just a technological evolution but a behavioral one. Users are being asked to adapt to an entirely new model of information engagement that blurs the lines between querying and exploring, between finding facts and building knowledge structures. This adaptation will occur gradually as interfaces prove their worth through improved outcomes and reduced cognitive overhead.

Users should expect the following key capabilities from conversational assistance systems: 

### Control and Transparency
The user must feel fully in control, not only where their work sits within the company, but also which topics to pursue further or abandon. As the user compiles information the interface must clearly indication of information sources and confidence levels of its importance to the project. With this transparency there then needs to be mechanisms to correct the system's understanding or redirect the conversation.

### Practical Integration
There needs to be clear delination of task and how discovery, gathering, and analysis comingle within the experience. This experience will not live in isolation but integrated into a larger experience, therefore it requires connections to the whole along with indications when specialized tools may be needed. These specialize tools can be rather broad as they may be autmated systems discovering addition information, setting reminders or jumping out of conversation and into traditional transactional flows. An ability to export insights or summaries from the conversation.

### Intelligent Assistance
Assistance/guidance is a delicate feature, finding a balance between too little and too much. Being proactive with suggestions of related areas to explore, recognizing implicit questions or unspoken information needs all without overwhelming or breaking contextual flow is difficult. 

### Natural Transitions
If need be, the user feel comfortable in the task they are performing and not be reminded what phase of work they are in. These phases are fluid and circular, it is about distinct experience task and not a rigidity of flow. Enabling smooth progressions from factual responses to guidance and exploration. Encouraging an ability to zoom in (specific details) and zoom out (broader context) without starting over. Support for non-linear conversation paths that mirror natural human inquiry

### Contextual Memory
The interface remembers previous exchanges without requiring repetition of context. Ability to reference earlier parts of the conversation naturally ("Tell me more about that first option"). Appropriate recall of user preferences and past decisions across sessions. Inclusion of projects and ability to move between them.

### Domain Expertise
Expectation of awareness of regulatory boundaries in sensitive domains like finance. Realizing certain domains have detailed industry-specific terminology and concepts which are not understood by most. The experience needs the flexibility to speak correctly but when required an ability to translate between technical jargon and everyday language

These expectations highlight the significant gap between current conversational interfaces and the ideal experience users envision—one that combines the natural flow of human conversation with the persistence and organization necessary for complex information tasks.


** 6. Conceptual Framework
The continuum of information-seeking behaviors
The information-seeking process is best understood not as discrete modes but as a fluid continuum where users move dynamically between different behaviors based on their evolving needs and understanding:
Known-Item Retrieval

Retrieving specific facts or documents already known to exist
Example: "What was the closing price of AAPL yesterday?"
Characteristics: High precision, immediate verification, minimal context needed

Directed Search

Seeking specific information whose exact form is unknown but whose parameters are clear
Example: "What are the tax implications of a Roth IRA conversion?"
Characteristics: Focused queries, evaluation of multiple results, moderate synthesis

Exploratory Search

Investigating a topic to understand its breadth and key components
Example: "Tell me about retirement planning options for self-employed individuals"
Characteristics: Broader queries, learning-oriented, pattern recognition across results

Problem-Solving Research

Gathering and synthesizing information to address a complex question or decision
Example: "How should I adjust my portfolio given current inflation and my retirement timeline?"
Characteristics: Multiple sessions, information comparison, integration with personal context

Knowledge Building

Developing expertise and mental models in a domain over extended periods
Example: "Help me understand modern portfolio theory and how it applies to my investments"
Characteristics: Deep engagement, critical evaluation, creation of personal knowledge structures

Users rarely stay within a single point on this continuum but move fluidly between modes—starting with directed searches that reveal the need for exploration, which then leads to problem-solving and knowledge building. Traditional interfaces typically force artificial boundaries between these modes, while conversational interfaces have the potential to support smooth transitions across the entire spectrum.
The challenge lies in designing systems that can recognize where users are on this continuum at any moment and adapt accordingly, without requiring explicit mode-switching or loss of context as information needs evolve.


Contextual signals that indicate user intent
## Contextual Signals that Indicate User Intent

Conversational interfaces can identify user positioning on the search-research continuum through various verbal and behavioral signals:

### Query Structure Signals
- **Query Length**: Longer, more complex queries typically indicate research mode
- **Question Types**: "What" and "when" questions suggest searching; "why" and "how" questions suggest researching
- **Specificity Level**: Highly specific queries ("S&P 500 closing value on March 10") versus broad topics ("current market trends")
- **Presence of Conditionals**: Phrases like "if," "would," and "might" signal exploratory thinking

### Temporal Indicators
- **Reference to Time Horizons**: Mentions of future planning or historical patterns suggest research mode
- **Session Duration**: Longer engagement indicates deeper research
- **Return Frequency**: Regular returns to the same topic area signal ongoing research
- **Pace of Interaction**: Rapid exchanges suggest searching; thoughtful pauses suggest processing/researching

### Conversation Flow Signals
- **Follow-up Question Patterns**: Series of increasingly specific questions indicates deepening research
- **Reference to Previous Information**: Connecting new queries to earlier exchanges
- **Comparative Language**: Using terms like "versus," "compared to," or "better than"
- **Integration of Personal Context**: Mentioning personal circumstances or goals

### Explicit Markers
- **Declared Purpose**: "I'm trying to decide..." or "I need to understand..."
- **Knowledge State Indicators**: "I already know..." versus "I'm just learning about..."
- **Time Investment Signals**: "I just need a quick answer" versus "I want to explore this topic"
- **Decision Proximity**: "I'm making this decision soon" versus "I'm just starting to think about this"

### Behavioral Signals
- **Engagement with Results**: Time spent reviewing information before next query
- **Saving/Bookmarking Actions**: Explicitly marking information as important
- **Information Re-examination**: Returning to previously provided information
- **Multi-modal Switching**: Moving between text, visualization, or calculation requests

By analyzing these signals in combination rather than isolation, conversational interfaces can develop a nuanced understanding of where users are in their information journey and adapt accordingly—providing concise answers for search-oriented queries while offering deeper exploration paths for research-oriented engagement.

Principles for designing adaptive conversational interfaces
## Principles for Designing Adaptive Conversational Interfaces

### Memory and Context Management
- **Progressive Context Building**: System should maintain and build upon conversation history without requiring users to repeat information
- **Selective Memory**: Not all exchanges carry equal weight; identify and prioritize pivotal information
- **Forgetting by Design**: Allow appropriate deprecation of outdated or rejected information paths

### Fluid Mode Adaptation
- **Seamless Transitions**: Support natural movement between search and research modes without explicit switching
- **Contextual Response Depth**: Adjust level of detail based on user's position in the information journey
- **Pace Matching**: Align system response tempo with user's information processing needs

### Transparent Intelligence
- **Visible Thinking**: Make the system's understanding and reasoning processes apparent to users
- **Confidence Signaling**: Clearly indicate certainty levels and information sources
- **Correctable Understanding**: Provide efficient mechanisms for users to refine the system's knowledge

### User Agency and Control
- **Navigable History**: Allow users to easily reference and return to previous points in the conversation
- **Explicit Importance Markers**: Enable users to highlight critical information or discard irrelevant paths
- **Customizable Depth**: Let users control the level of detail and exploration

### Cognitive Support Functions
- **Pattern Recognition**: Help users identify connections across information gathered over time
- **Summary and Synthesis**: Periodically consolidate key learnings and insights
- **Alternative Perspectives**: Present multiple viewpoints to support critical thinking

### Workflow Integration
- **Session Continuity**: Support multi-session research activities with minimal re-establishment of context
- **Export and Share**: Enable capturing insights in forms usable outside the conversation
- **Handoff Design**: Create smooth transitions between conversational and traditional interfaces when appropriate

### Learning and Evolution
- **Adaptation to User Style**: Learn individual users' communication patterns and preferences
- **Domain-Specific Tuning**: Adjust interaction patterns based on subject matter context
- **Progressive Disclosure**: Evolve capabilities as users become more sophisticated in their usage

These principles provide a framework for designing conversational interfaces that can effectively support the full spectrum of information-seeking behaviors, adapting dynamically to user needs while maintaining the natural flow of human conversation. The most effective implementations will balance sophisticated adaptation with predictable, transparent behavior that builds user trust and competence over time.

Proposed model for dynamic mode-switching
## Proposed Model for Dynamic Mode-Switching

A robust model for dynamic mode-switching in conversational interfaces should operate seamlessly without requiring explicit user commands while still providing transparency and control. This proposed framework encompasses both the detection mechanisms and adaptation strategies:

### Three-Layer Detection Framework

**1. Real-Time Indicators**
- Analyze immediate linguistic patterns (question types, specificity, complexity)
- Monitor interaction patterns (response reading time, follow-up speed)
- Assess explicit user signals (stated goals, time constraints)

**2. Session Context Layer**
- Track evolution of query specificity and complexity over time
- Identify thematic connections between questions
- Recognize deepening engagement within topic areas

**3. User Profile Layer**
- Build understanding of individual's typical interaction patterns
- Recognize domain expertise levels and adjust accordingly
- Remember previously established research territories

### Mode-Switching Triggers

**Searching → Researching Triggers:**
- Multiple related queries within same domain
- Increasing specificity after broad initial questions
- Extended engagement with provided information
- Explicit statements of deeper interest or confusion

**Researching → Searching Triggers:**
- Sharp topic changes indicating completion of research thread
- Explicit time constraints or urgency markers
- Simple, direct questions amid complex exploration
- Decreased engagement with detailed responses

### Adaptive Response Framework

**Content Adaptation:**
- Adjust information density based on detected mode
- Scale complexity of vocabulary and concepts
- Modify level of detail in explanations
- Adapt citation and source transparency

**Interaction Pattern Shifts:**
- Increase proactive suggestions in research mode
- Offer synthesizing summaries at appropriate intervals
- Provide navigational cues for complex information landscapes
- Adjust confirmation and clarification frequency

**Visual/Interface Adaptations:**
- Highlight relationship between current and previous exchanges
- Show thematic grouping of information in research mode
- Provide progress indicators for extended explorations
- Enable explicit bookmarking and importance marking

### Implementation Considerations

This model should operate on a continuous spectrum rather than binary states, with weighted signals determining the system's position between pure search and deep research modes. The adaptation should be gradual and natural, avoiding jarring transitions while still providing sufficient differentiation in system behavior to support different information needs.

Critically, the system should maintain "mode awareness" transparency, subtly indicating its current understanding of user intent without disrupting the conversation flow. This allows users to implicitly or explicitly correct the system's mode assessment when needed.

## Designing for Memory and Context Preservation

### Core Principles

**Hierarchical Memory Structure**
- Implement a multi-tiered memory system that distinguishes between:

  - Supporting information (relevant details, explored options)
  - Contextual backdrop (general domain knowledge, earlier explorations)

- Maintain recency and relevance scoring for information
- Design decay functions that preserve critical information longer
- Create mechanisms to resurface older information when it becomes relevant again

**Visual Context Representation**
- Provide subtle indicators of conversation history and structure
- Create accessible visualizations of the research journey
- Allow users to navigate through previous context easily

### Implementation Strategies

**Conversation Summarization**
- Generate periodic summaries of key findings and decisions
- Surface these summaries at strategic points (session starts, topic shifts)
- Allow users to correct or refine these summaries

**Explicit Bookmarking and Tagging**
- Enable users to flag important information for persistence
- Provide lightweight tagging capabilities for organizational purposes
- Offer "pin this" functionality for critical facts that should always be considered

**Context Reactivation Prompts**
- Design natural ways to reference previous conversations ("Last time we discussed...")

- Provide opportunities for users to manually reincorporate older context

**Project-Based Organization**
- Group related conversations into persistent projects or themes
- Allow cross-referencing between related research areas

**Progressive Context Loading**

**Cross-Session Persistence**

Effective memory and context preservation should feel invisible when working correctly, supporting natural conversation flow while eliminating the cognitive burden of manually maintaining context across complex information-seeking journeys.

## Natural Mode Transitions

### Principles of Natural Transitions

**Gradual Adaptation**
- Implement subtle shifts in response style rather than abrupt changes
- Blend characteristics of search and research modes during transitional phases
- Avoid jarring interface changes that disrupt user flow

**Contextual Cue Provision**
- Offer gentle guidance when sensing mode transitions ("Would you like to explore this further?")
- Provide navigation options that support deeper investigation without forcing it
- Use linguistic bridges that acknowledge the shifting information need

**Bidirectional Flexibility**
- Support fluid movement between deeper research and quick fact-checking
- Allow temporary mode shifts without losing the broader research context
- Enable users to "zoom in" on specifics and "zoom out" to bigger picture

### Implementation Approaches

**Linguistic Transition Markers**
- Use subtle language shifts to signal mode changes
- In transition to research: "That's the basic answer. Some additional context that might be helpful..."
- In transition to search: "In summary, the specific answer is..."

**Progressive Information Layering**
- Structure responses with essential facts first, followed by deeper context
- Allow users to naturally engage with the level that matches their need
- Design expandable content that reveals more detail as interest deepens

**Conversational Pivot Techniques**
- Recognize potential inflection points in user information needs
- Offer natural continuations that support mode transitions
- Acknowledge shifts in complexity or depth when they occur

**Visual and Interaction Shifts**
- Introduce subtle visual cues that indicate current mode (without requiring explicit labels)
- Adjust interaction patterns gradually as users transition between modes
- Provide consistent anchors that remain stable across transitions

### Avoiding Common Pitfalls

**Premature Mode Assumptions**
- Validate mode transition signals before significant adaptation
- Allow grace periods of ambiguity during transitions
- Provide escape routes if the system misinterprets signals

**Overloading Transitions**
- Avoid introducing too many new elements during mode shifts
- Maintain core interaction patterns while adapting peripheral elements
- Ensure users always understand how to navigate and control the experience

**Transition Fatigue**
- Reduce transition overhead for frequent mode-switchers
- Create efficient patterns for users who regularly alternate between modes
- Design for experienced users who may switch modes rapidly

Natural mode transitions should feel intuitive and supportive, anticipating user needs while avoiding presumptive behavior. The ideal experience maintains conversation flow while subtly adapting to evolving information needs, creating a sense of a thoughtful partner rather than a system with distinct "modes."
